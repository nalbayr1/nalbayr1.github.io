Project 1

offical publication

Motivation

  • Better gauge of performance in armor ceramics
  • Projectile depth affected by rapid granular flow
  • Understand rearrangement of particles in a rapid granular flow
  • Analyze force networks at different time steps
❮ ❯

Abstract

In order to better gauge performance in armor ceramics, we can study the granular flow of particles as a projectile makes impact. Granular flow can affect a projectile's depth and final placement. As impact is made within the material, a comminuted zone is created. This zone is the area in which we analyze force networks at different time steps. When studying temporal changes in force networks, we are dealing with different nodes and edges carrying information such as location (x, y, z), contact forces, radi, etc. We are able to label assortments of these nodes and edges based on the information they carry. Through the contact force magnitude found in edges and angles created between triplets of nodes, we label them as either kinks (when the angle found between triplets is less than 135 degrees), extensions (when the angle found between triplets is greater than or equal to 135 degrees), merges (a new edge is found between 2 nodes), or disappearance (edge or node is no longer present). With this information, we hope to find correlations between the labels to better understand particle rearrangement.

Research

Throughout our work, we have focused on accessing and analyzing data using a robust network framework. This has allowed us to generate informative graphs that depict changes in node and edge positions over different time steps. We store comprehensive information for each time step, including nodes and edges, enabling us to perform various comparisons and identify new nodes and disappearing nodes between time steps. By assigning unique numbers to nodes, we create arrays that represent nodes at specific time steps, facilitating calculations for direction, angles, normal force, tangential force, and magnitude. Our labeling process ensures accurate identification of triplets and pairs of nodes, considering contact force magnitudes and selecting the most significant edges. Additionally, we distinguish between "kinks" and "extensions" based on the defined angles. To optimize efficiency, we create unique arrays to avoid redundant iterations. Our ultimate objective is to establish a correlation between particle dynamics and network changes, providing valuable insights into granular flow. Through in-depth analysis and comparison of labeled nodes and edges, we aim to test label randomness and enhance our understanding of granular flow phenomena.
